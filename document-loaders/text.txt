The Basics of Coral Reefs

Coral reefs are among the most diverse ecosystems on Earth. Although they cover less than one percent of the ocean floor, they provide habitat for roughly a quarter of all marine species.

Corals are small animals called polyps. These polyps live in colonies and build hard calcium carbonate skeletons over time. As generations of corals grow and die, their skeletons accumulate and form the reef structures we see today.

Coral reefs are often called the "rainforests of the sea" because of the incredible variety of life they support. Fish, crustaceans, mollusks, and many other organisms depend on reefs for food, shelter, and breeding grounds.

Reefs also benefit humans. They help protect coastlines from waves and storms, support fishing industries, and attract tourists. Many communities around the world rely on healthy reef ecosystems for their livelihoods.

However, coral reefs face significant threats. Rising ocean temperatures can cause coral bleaching, a process in which corals expel the algae that live within their tissues. Pollution, overfishing, and ocean acidification also contribute to reef decline.

Scientists and conservation organizations are working to protect and restore coral reefs through marine protected areas, sustainable fishing practices, and coral restoration projects. Preserving reefs is important not only for marine biodiversity but also for the millions of people who depend on them.
